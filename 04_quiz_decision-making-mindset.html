Test Your Decision-Making:
Applying the Mindset

Six urban scenarios. For each, identify which professional posture the situation calls for — and why.

ProgressQuestion 1 of 6

Question 1

Scenario: A city's emergency response times have increased 35% over two years. You have full access to data, the problem scope is clear, and department heads agree something must change.

Which posture leads the response?

Consider which posture is built for bounded, evidence-available, goal-agreed problems.

Question 2

Scenario: A major urban regeneration plan has strong technical backing. But several community groups feel the plan ignores the lived experience of long-term residents. The data is good. The trust is not.

Which posture does this situation primarily call for?

The technical problem is solved. Something else needs attention.

Question 3

Scenario: There is enormous political pressure to approve a 30-year waste management concession. The financial terms are attractive. But the contract would lock the city into a model that makes circular economy transition nearly impossible.

Which posture must be activated — even at political cost?

This is about what happens to the city long after the decision-makers have moved on.

Question 4

Scenario: Civic engagement metrics look healthy — voter turnout is stable, community meetings are well-attended. But three experienced community organisers have separately told you they feel something has "gone quiet" in the city. Residents seem present but disengaged.

What does this situation require — and why?

The data says one thing. The people who know the city say another.

Question 5

Scenario: Two city departments — health and housing — have been collecting data separately for five years. No one has ever compared the datasets. A junior analyst suspects that overlaying the data would reveal a spatial pattern linking housing instability to chronic illness that neither department has seen.

Which posture unlocks this situation?

The insight already exists in the data — it just needs the right move to surface it.

Question 6

Scenario: A city is designing a major new mobility policy. The technical team is excellent. The community consultation was thorough. The long-term implications have been considered. But the policy team is about to launch without asking: which neighbourhood will experience the negative spillover effects first?

Which posture catches this blind spot?

Everything looks right. But something important hasn't been asked.
