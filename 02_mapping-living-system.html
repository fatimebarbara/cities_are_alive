The four flows

A city's intelligence lives not in what stands still, but in what moves. Tap each flow to explore what it reveals in your city.

🚶
People

The movement of residents, commuters, visitors, migrants. The living pulse of the city.

  • Daily commuting patterns and where they break down
  • Which neighbourhoods people avoid — and why
  • Migration routes and how they reshape identity
  • Tourism flows and their effects on locals

In your city: where do people flow freely, and where do they get stuck?

📡
Information

Data, signals, rumours, alerts, trust. The nervous system of the city.

  • Weak signals: rumours, social media bursts, trust shifts
  • Data infrastructure: what is measured and what is ignored
  • Institutional communication and its failures
  • Misinformation as a city system event

In your city: what information do residents trust — and what gets ignored?

⚡
Resources

Energy, water, goods, capital, materials. The metabolic system of the city.

  • Supply chains and their vulnerabilities
  • Energy consumption patterns and inequality
  • Food access and distribution across neighbourhoods
  • Financial flows: investment, extraction, circulation

In your city: who has reliable access to resources — and who doesn't?

💬
Emotions

Collective mood, trust, pride, fear, tension, belonging. The immune system of the city.

  • Neighbourhood-level trust in institutions
  • Sense of safety and public dignity
  • Community solidarity and social cohesion
  • Collective grief, pride, and aspiration

In your city: which emotional flows are most fragile right now?

Map your city

Now apply the frameworks to your own context. Use these prompts to build your personalised living system map.

Your living city field notes

Take your time. There are no correct answers — only honest observations.

1. Which flow is most visibly strained in your city right now?

People, information, resources, or emotions — and what does that strain look like in practice?

2. Describe one feedback loop you can observe in your city.

What is the original pressure, what response does it trigger, and what does that response cause next?

3. Pick one visible urban problem in your city. What are three other systems it is connected to?

Follow the interdependencies — each connection reveals something invisible about the system.

4. What is one weak signal you have noticed recently — something small that might indicate a bigger pattern?

A shift in behaviour, a conversation, a change you almost dismissed.
